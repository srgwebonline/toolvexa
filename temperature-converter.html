Celsius Scale

Created by Anders Celsius in 1742. Based on water properties: 0°C for freezing point and 100°C for boiling point at sea level.

Fahrenheit Scale

Invented by Daniel Gabriel Fahrenheit in 1724. Originally based on three reference points including human body temperature.

Kelvin Scale

Absolute thermodynamic temperature scale. 0 K is absolute zero where molecular motion stops. Used in scientific calculations.

Frequently Asked Questions

How accurate is the temperature conversion?

Our temperature converter uses precise mathematical formulas for conversion between all temperature scales. The calculations are accurate to the decimal point specified by the user's input.

What's the formula for Celsius to Fahrenheit?

The formula is: °F = (°C × 9/5) + 32. For example, to convert 20°C to Fahrenheit: (20 × 9/5) + 32 = 68°F.

What is absolute zero in different scales?

Absolute zero, the lowest possible temperature, is:
• -273.15°C (Celsius)
• -459.67°F (Fahrenheit)
• 0 K (Kelvin)
• 0°R (Rankine)

Why are there different temperature scales?

Different scales were developed for different purposes: Celsius for scientific use, Fahrenheit for everyday weather, Kelvin for absolute temperature measurements in science, and Rankine for engineering applications.

Can I convert negative temperatures?

Yes! Our converter handles negative temperatures for all scales. For example, -40°C equals -40°F, which is where the two scales intersect.

Temperature Scale Information

Celsius (°C)

Most widely used temperature scale worldwide. Based on water's freezing (0°C) and boiling (100°C) points at sea level.

Fahrenheit (°F)

Primarily used in the United States. Water freezes at 32°F and boils at 212°F.

Kelvin (K)

Absolute temperature scale used in scientific contexts. 0 K is absolute zero (-273.15°C).

Rankine (°R)

Absolute temperature scale used in some engineering fields in the United States.
